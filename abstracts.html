Conference Abstracts

The “Fearless Teaching Framework” is a conceptual model of an effective course that emphasizes four pillars that, working in tandem, promote student achievement and learning. These pillars, climate, content, practices and assessment, are evidenced in the accomplishments of our presenters.

Reshaping Perceptions of Large Lecture Classes

Patricia Alexander, Anish Singh and Hailey Gibbs, Human Development & Quantitative Methodology Department

EDHD 201, Learning How to Learn, is a class created to provide UMD undergraduates opportunities to acquire habits of mind and habits of action associated with optimal learning by engaging in experiences that foster those habits. Being taught for the first time as a large enrollment class (100+ students), this innovative course employs a physical structure and pedagogical techniques that seem incompatible with such courses. To support its unique structure and pedagogy, EDHD 201 is being held in Edward St. John Learning and Teaching Center that makes such innovations possible. Due to the experiences being instituted, it is expected that participation in this non-traditional course should challenge students' perceptions of the foundational character of large enrollment classes. To test that assumption, enrolled students complete a pre-assessment exploring their views of the large enrollment classes they have taken, Students' Perceptions of Large Enrollment Classes (S-PLEC). This assessment addresses the four areas of the Fearless Teaching Framework: climate, content, practices, and assessments. At the conclusion of the course, students will again be asked to complete a version of this assessment that not only asks about their perceptions of large enrollment classes in general but about EDHD 201 in particular.

Because the organization and pedagogy associated with EDHD 201 are aligned with the dimensions of the Fearless Teaching Framework it is expected that the perceptions of large enrollment classes in general and this particular course will differ significantly on all four dimensions of the Framework. For example, efforts are made to ensure that students feel welcomed and their backgrounds and interests reflected in activities and discussions (climate). The content of the course is made personally relevant to students (content), who learn by engaging in well-researched activities (practices). Finally, assessments are constructed to require the application of learnings with well-articulated rubrics to guide performance and self-evaluation.

Using Content and Task-based Teaching to Address Grammar More Effectively

Heather Mehrtens, Maryland English Institute (MEI)

In many grammar classes, the present-practice-produce, or PPP, method is used; however, research shows that this method does not best develop successful language skills, because it promotes explicit over implicit knowledge (Ellis 1995; Ellis, 2003). In addition, cognitive science shows that there is a clear limit to how much information students can retain from such instruction (Anderson, 2000; Ellis, 2003; Kennedy, 2006). The primary goal of this session is to show attendees how to flip a grammar lesson, i.e. by having students complete grammar tasks in small groups (e.g., by analyzing sentences and texts) to elicit the target (grammatical) structure.

Fearless Teaching: A mid-semester evaluation tool

Alice Donlan and Virginia Byrne, Teaching and Learning Transformation Center

Last year, the Teaching and Learning Transformation Center (TLTC) unveiled the Fearless Teaching Framework, a conceptual framework that identifies the TLTC's theory of change and definition of effective teaching. Specifically, the TLTC conceptualizes effective teaching as that which attends to excellent classroom climate, course content, teaching practices, and assessment strategies. These four pieces of effective teaching reflect decades of education research and theory (e.g., Hattie & Timperley, 2007; Rowland, 2014; Wigfield & Eccles, 2000) and distill that work into constructs that instructors can incorporate into their classrooms.

As a next step, we are connecting our conceptualization of effective teaching with our evaluation of effective teaching. The research team at the TLTC has constructed a teaching evaluation that is aligned with the Fearless Teaching Framework. In collaboration with education and pedagogical experts on campus, we constructed a short scale to assess students' perceptions of the course climate, content, teaching practices, and assessment, as well as items on basic course functioning. These items were designed to be used mid-semester to gather formative feedback from students.

We will present psychometric data analysis on two rounds of scale piloting. We will also discuss best practices for student evaluations of teaching.

Brainstorm Your Way to Creative Assignment Topics!

Karen Lamb

Students often find the most difficult part of getting started with an assignment is identifying a topic that excites and enthuses them. Brainstorming can help these students generate topics for a speech or English paper, spark ideas for a creative marketing strategy, and even to think of innovative designs for building a robot.

The premise of brainstorming is that when a large number of ideas and/or suggestions are produced, high-quality solutions will emerge. There are two steps in the brainstorming process. In the creative stage, participants suggest as many ideas as possible in the shortest amount of time; the refrain is quantity over quality. In the evaluative stage, participants eliminate unworkable ideas and prioritize the best ideas.

As I move around the classroom to assist students on their personal projects, it is evident that students some students are able to identify their research needs, and others seem less directed.

Beyond the Lecture: Strategies to Engage Students, Model Professional Skills and Promote Student Learning

Theresa Nebel Robinson, Teaching and Learning, Policy and Leadership Department

"Student engagement is the product of motivation and active learning... " shares Elizabeth F. Barkley, author of Student Engagement Techniques: A Handbook for College Faculty. As facilitators for student learning and models of professional skills, instructors in higher education must incorporate effective learning strategies that engage students in learning and take them beyond the mere acquisition of concepts and ideas.

In 2001, Lorin W Anderson and David R Krathwohl worked with a team to revise the cognitive domains outlined by Benjamin Bloom in his Taxonomy of Educational Objectives. The overview includes the facets of remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate and create for use in learning, teaching, and assessment. These revisions also allow for the extension of Bloom's Taxonomy beyond determining teaching objectives to the inclusion of methods for learning and assessment for student success.

Incorporating the ACME approach to course planning, we will identify effective practices for incorporating Analysis, Collaboration, Movement, and Explanation to university classes for a more intensive approach to student learning. Through the focus on purposeful planning that encourages student motivation and active learning we will: examine 3-4 instructional strategies for each ACME domain; discuss methods for application of ACME within varied subjects; consider effective planning models for course progression.

No-frills Virtual Whiteboard Technology: How, and what for?

Omer Preminger, Linguistics Department

Using a relatively inexpensive piece of software, anyone with a Windows- or iOS-based tablet can use their tablet as a wireless virtual whiteboard in any classroom with the most basic VGA or HDMI projection capabilities. This provides several advantages when compared with more traditional alternatives. First, the instructor is no longer "tethered" to the front of the class / lecture hall; you can write on the board while walking around the classroom. Second, the instructor can "rewind" (or "fast forward") the whiteboard so that, for example, if a student asks a question about something that was on the board earlier and no longer is, the instructor can rewind the board back to that point in the class, and then fast-forward back to where they were once they're done addressing the issue. Third, at the end of the session, the instructor can seamlessly provide the students with a PDF of everything that was on the board throughout the class.

Phil Donahue, Richard Dawson, or Catchbox: Students giving answers

Evan Golub, Computer Science Department

Three techniques can be thought of as the "Phil Donahue" where the faculty member walks out into the room and hands a microphone to a student, the "Richard Dawson" where the student tells their answer to the faculty member and the faculty member repeats it to the class, and the "Catchbox" where the faculty member (or other students) toss a throwable microphone to a student. We will quickly experience and discuss each of them, and learn about some student opinions, in this lightning round session.

Instructor Behaviors Promote Students' Sense of Belongingness

Junjie Su, RH Smith Office of Transformational Learning

Given that students' sense of "belongingness" is a strong indicator of their success in college (Freeman et al., 2007; Johnson et al., 2007; Strayhorn, 2012; Yorke, 2016), we examined whether there was anything that instructors could do to promote this sense of "belongingness" in college students. We asked college students (N = 261) to rate their instructor's behavior after recalling either a favorite or a least favorite class, and also asked them to rate their perceived sense of "belongingness". Consistent with our expectations, we found evidence supporting the claim that patterns (profiles) of instructor behavior is positively correlated with students' sense of "belongingness". However, we also found that the relationship between instructors' behavioral profiles and students' sense of "belongingness" is moderated by student demographic variables, such as their race, gender, and year of study.

Teaching Students to Recognize Systemic Racism in Archives

Cecelia Vetter, Laura Cleary and Ashleigh Coren, University of Maryland Libraries

As the Instruction Team at a special collections library we provide one-shot instruction sessions to undergraduate classes and teach students how to analyze and incorporate primary sources into their research papers. Building on our students' growing awareness of civil unrest in the United States, we incorporated discussions of historical and archival bias, which help students think critically about archival holdings and historical research.

In class students conduct a document analysis, attempt to define the historical context in which the object exists, and to consider racism, sexism or other types of prejudice that might influence the creation of the object. We question how secondary sources may represent biased narratives and that by exploring primary sources, students can find historically silenced voices. Additionally, archivists and librarians experience implicit bias, which impacts item selection and description. Working through these questions, students learn the processes that lead to the underrepresentation of minority voices in archival collections. Students then propose ways to remedy past mistakes and improve the representation of minority voices.

We have experienced successes and challenges engaging students in these conversations. This poster examines our experience and dissects our processes for implementing discussions of systemic and implicit bias into our classes.

Using Twitter to Assess Campus Climate after a Hate Crime

Bridget L. Higginbotham, School of Public Health; Virginia Byrne, Teaching and Learning, Policy and Leadership; and Alice Donlan, Teaching and Learning Transformation Center

After the racially-motivated murder of Lt. Richard Collins III, students used Twitter to share personal experiences of being afraid on campus. This study analyzed publicly available tweets to see where (e.g., classrooms) and with whom (e.g., instructors, peers) people experienced instances in which they did not feel safe.

The first and second authors collected tweets from public accounts that tweeted using the hashtag #FearTheTurtle during May or June 2017. We reviewed the 785 relevant tweets and inductively coded them as opinions vs. personal experiences. We inductively coded the experience tweets (N=225) in up to two emergent categories that represented experiences with bias-related activities in an academic setting (N = 66), with police (N = 30), with peers or in social settings (N = 32), on campus (N = 63), and other (N= 53). Our Cohen's kappa was .761. We discussed all differences and came to agreement on all codes.

Our approach to hearing and amplifying the voices of marginalized students contributes to how educational institutions might consider social media analysis to understand student sense of safety and belonging. This analysis provides campus leaders with insight into where on campus students experience undue stress and instances of bias.

Unconference: build a strong learning community through participant-driven professional development

Natalie Janiszewski and Melanee Bryant, R.H. Smith Office of Transformational Learning

Unlike traditional conferences, which have schedules set months in advance by the people running the convention, an unconference has an agenda that is created by the participants at the start of the event. Instead of presentations, people are encouraged to have discussions and hands-on sessions. Built on principles of connected and participatory learning, unconferences strive to bring participants together to talk about the things that matter most to them: their interests, passions, and questions. Unconference attendees can choose to lead sessions on those things that matter, with an expectation that the people in the room will work together to build understanding by sharing their own knowledge and questions. Unconferences are free for participants and reliant on the "rule of two feet" that empowers everyone to find sessions that meet their needs.

Redesigning Classes to Promote Student Learning and Positive Perceptions of Science Through Active Learning

Sara Lombardi, Biology Department

Impact of class size on student learning is a topic of broad interest, but predominantly focus on small class sizes (15-30 students) in public K-12 settings. Few studies focus on differences between medium to large classes (50-300 student) in higher education setting. Often, the differences in learning gains between truly large and small classes are attributed to the learning difference due to the activities in the classroom -- smaller classes are more conducive to active learning and student-centric learning. The goal of our research is to assess the impact of class size when controlling for curriculum and instructor across a range of student class sizes. This is an important piece of information as "at risk" students benefit more from small class sizes Therefore, if the benefits of small classes can be achieved while still maintaining large class sizes, which can be necessary at large universities, it can have wide-ranging implications to a variety of courses across institutions.

Real-time Data Improves Climate Literacy of Middle and High Schoolers

Emily B. Starobin, Environmental Science and Policy

Local real-time data is a valuable tool in cultivating a climate and data literate student population. Classroom activities that incorporate real-time data (RTD) engage students in ocean science in a way that increases subject relevance and fosters environmental stewardship. Moreover, these activities invite students to interact with data like scientists by asking questions, analyzing data, and engaging in arguments from evidence. As such, they are instrumental in supporting Science and Engineering Practices (SEPs) outlined in the Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS). Unfortunately, non-expert audiences such as teachers are not able to take full advantage of these RTD resources. The present project focuses on the development of detailed, place-based lesson plans for middle and high school teachers to facilitate classroom use of oceanic real-time data. The lesson plans utilize online data portals such as the National Estuarine Research Reserve System (NERRS) Central Data Management Office to demonstrate important ocean science principles. Content areas covered by these lesson plans range from hurricane indicators, both oceanographic and atmospheric to the coastal daily winds and rains phenomenon.

Climate science teachers must be better equipped with clear, flexible, and user-friendly educational materials to feel comfortable using local real-time data in the classroom.
